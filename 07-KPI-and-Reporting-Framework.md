# 07 - KPI and Reporting Framework

## We Didn't Build Reports for Management. We Built Reports to Answer Questions.

One mistake I've seen in many reporting projects is starting with charts and dashboards before understanding what people actually need to know.

Our reporting requirements developed in the opposite direction.

Most reports existed because someone in the business was trying to answer a specific operational question.

For example:

* Which marketing channels are generating inquiries?
* Which destinations are customers asking about most frequently?
* Are consultants creating leads consistently?
* How many leads are actually becoming customers?
* Which services are performing better?
* Where are we losing opportunities?

The reporting framework evolved gradually as those questions became more important.

---

# Looking at the Entire Funnel

One of the main goals was connecting marketing activity to business outcomes.

We were not interested in measuring traffic alone.

A campaign generating thousands of visitors had little value if it failed to generate inquiries or customers.

As a result, we tried to monitor multiple stages of the customer journey.

A simplified version of the funnel looked like this:

Marketing Activity

↓

Landing Page Visits

↓

Call-To-Action Engagement

↓

Phone Calls

↓

CRM Leads

↓

Follow-Up Activities

↓

Customers

This structure helped us understand where performance was strong and where improvements were needed.

Instead of saying:

"Marketing isn't working"

or

"Sales isn't working"

we could identify the specific stage where performance was declining.

---

# Marketing Performance Visibility

Alongside CRM reporting, we also maintained marketing planning and performance tracking sheets.

These reports helped compare planned marketing investments against actual activity and visitor volumes.

Typical channels included:

* Google Ads
* Google Display Network
* SEO
* Email Marketing
* SMS Campaigns
* Instagram Activities
* Content Production
* Banner Advertising
* Link Building

Rather than evaluating channels based only on cost, we wanted to understand how they contributed to the larger acquisition process.

The objective was to create visibility between marketing effort and customer acquisition.

---

# Lead Registration KPIs

One of the earliest KPIs we monitored focused on lead creation.

Because incoming calls were integrated with the CRM through Issabel, we could compare:

Unique Calls

versus

CRM Leads Created

This became an important operational metric.

The objective was not to pressure consultants.

The objective was to ensure customer inquiries were being captured consistently.

Without lead registration, reporting quality deteriorated quickly.

The KPI helped identify situations where customer interactions were occurring but not being reflected in the CRM.

Looking back, it was a simple metric, but it provided significant operational visibility.

---

# Qualification and Customer Insight Reporting

The qualification framework created opportunities for much richer reporting than simple lead counts.

Because different information was collected for ticket, tour, and visa inquiries, management could begin identifying patterns that previously remained hidden.

Examples included:

* Most requested destinations
* Travel seasonality trends
* Service demand patterns
* Customer profile distribution
* Travel history characteristics
* Financial capability segmentation

Over time, this information became useful not only for sales activities but also for planning and decision-making.

The CRM gradually became a source of business intelligence rather than simply a customer database.

---

# Conversion Tracking

One of the most valuable areas of reporting involved conversion visibility.

Understanding how many leads became customers was far more useful than simply counting inquiries.

The funnel helped answer questions such as:

* Which channels generated the best opportunities?
* Which services converted most effectively?
* Where were opportunities being lost?
* Which stages required improvement?

When conversion rates declined, the data helped narrow the investigation.

The issue might be:

* Marketing quality
* Lead qualification
* Follow-up execution
* Customer expectations
* Operational constraints

Without visibility into the funnel, all of these situations would look similar.

---

# Destination and Demand Analysis

One reporting area that became particularly useful involved customer demand analysis.

Because destination information was collected during qualification, we could observe trends across customer inquiries.

Questions such as:

* Which destinations are generating the most demand?
* How does demand change throughout the year?
* Which services are becoming more popular?
* What customer segments are emerging?

became easier to answer.

This information supported both marketing activities and operational planning.

It also helped management better understand customer behavior over time.

---

# Reporting Beyond the CRM

Although Microsoft Dynamics CRM was the primary source of operational data, not every report lived inside the CRM.

In some situations, I maintained additional reporting and analysis through Google Sheets.

These reports were typically used to combine information from multiple sources and provide a broader view of marketing and operational performance.

The goal was never to create reports for the sake of reporting.

The goal was to create visibility.

Sometimes the CRM was the best tool.

Sometimes a spreadsheet provided more flexibility.

The important thing was ensuring decision-makers had access to useful information.

---

# Supporting Management Decisions

One of the biggest benefits of the reporting framework was improving the quality of operational discussions.

Before reliable reporting, many conversations depended on assumptions.

Different departments often had different interpretations of performance.

Once funnel metrics, lead metrics, marketing data, and customer information became available, discussions became more objective.

Instead of asking:

"What do we think is happening?"

we could begin asking:

"What does the data tell us?"

This did not eliminate uncertainty, but it significantly improved visibility.

---

# Lessons Learned

Looking back, one lesson stands out more than any other.

The most valuable KPI is not necessarily the most complex KPI.

Some of the most useful metrics were surprisingly simple:

* Calls
* Leads
* Follow-ups
* Conversions

When those metrics were connected together, they provided a much clearer picture of business performance than many complicated dashboards.

Another lesson was that reporting should support decisions.

Collecting data is easy.

Creating visibility that helps people take action is much harder.

The reporting framework gradually evolved because the organization kept asking practical questions and looking for better answers.

In many ways, that process was more important than any individual report or dashboard.
