# 01 - Business Context

## Before CRM, We Had a Visibility Problem

When I look back at our CRM journey, I don't think the main problem was customer management.

The real problem was visibility.

Around 2016–2017, ParastooSeir was already active across multiple travel services including airline tickets, tours, and visa applications. At the same time, our digital marketing activities were growing. We were investing in SEO, Google Ads, social media, landing pages, SMS campaigns, and email marketing to attract potential customers.

The good news was that inquiries were increasing.

The difficult part was understanding what happened after those inquiries reached the company.

A customer might arrive from Google Ads, visit a landing page, call the office, speak with a consultant, receive information, and then disappear. In many cases we could not easily answer simple questions such as:

* How many calls are we receiving?
* How many of those calls become leads?
* Which destinations are customers asking about most frequently?
* Which marketing channels generate the highest quality leads?
* How effectively are consultants following up with potential customers?
* Where are we losing customers in the process?

Everyone had part of the story, but nobody had the complete picture.

---

# Multiple Services, Different Customer Journeys

One of the challenges of working in a travel business is that not all customers are the same.

Someone looking for a flight ticket has very different needs compared to someone planning an international tour or applying for a visa.

Each service required different information.

For example, a ticket inquiry might focus on destination, travel date, and ticket class.

A tour inquiry required information about destination, preferred travel period, and hotel expectations.

Visa inquiries were even more complicated because factors such as financial capability, employment status, travel history, and marital status could influence the type of advice provided.

As the number of inquiries increased, it became clear that we needed a more structured way to capture and organize this information.

---

# Marketing Was Generating Traffic, But We Needed More Than Traffic

At the time, most of our campaigns were designed around a simple call-to-action:

"Call the office."

Whether traffic came from Google, social media, SMS campaigns, or landing pages, the objective was usually the same: encourage potential customers to speak directly with a travel consultant.

That approach worked well because travel services often require consultation before purchase.

However, it also created a new challenge.

Once the phone rang, how could we measure what happened next?

Without a structured process, it was difficult to connect marketing activities to actual business outcomes.

We could see traffic.

We could see calls.

But understanding how those calls turned into leads and customers was much harder.

---

# Why We Started Looking at CRM Solutions

The decision to evaluate CRM systems was not driven by technology alone.

We were trying to solve operational problems.

We wanted a way to:

* Register inquiries consistently
* Track customer interactions
* Improve follow-up discipline
* Measure consultant activity
* Understand conversion performance
* Build better reporting
* Create visibility for management

After researching different options available at the time, Microsoft Dynamics CRM was selected as the platform that best matched our requirements.

The goal was never to use CRM as a digital address book.

The goal was to build a process around customer acquisition and customer management.

---

# Thinking Beyond Sales

One thing that often gets overlooked when people talk about CRM projects is that the software itself is only a small part of the story.

What mattered more was the process around it.

As discussions progressed, it became clear that the CRM needed to support much more than lead registration.

We wanted to create a structure that included:

* Lead qualification
* Follow-up activities
* Customer satisfaction measurement
* Happy calls
* Complaint management
* Marketing performance analysis
* Sales funnel visibility

This meant CRM would eventually become connected to multiple departments rather than being used only by sales consultants.

---

# Building a More Measurable Customer Journey

One of the biggest goals behind the project was creating visibility across the customer journey.

We wanted to better understand how customers moved through the business.

A simplified version looked something like this:

Marketing Activity

↓

Landing Page Visit

↓

Phone Call

↓

Lead Registration

↓

Follow-Up

↓

Customer

↓

Customer Satisfaction

For the first time, we were trying to measure each stage rather than focusing only on final sales numbers.

This allowed us to identify where improvements were needed.

Sometimes the issue was marketing.

Sometimes it was follow-up.

Sometimes it was lead qualification.

Without visibility, all of these problems looked the same.

---

# Looking Back

Looking back now, I don't see this as a CRM implementation project.

I see it as an attempt to make customer acquisition and customer management more measurable.

The software was important, but the bigger challenge was creating processes that people would actually use and that management could rely on when making decisions.

Many of the lessons learned during this period later influenced reporting initiatives, workflow improvements, customer follow-up practices, and eventually the decision to move away from Microsoft Dynamics and adopt a different CRM platform.

That migration would come later.

The first challenge was simply creating visibility where very little existed before.
